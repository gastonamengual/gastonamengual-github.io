Descriptive Statistics

Statistics Mon 25 January 2021

Notebook written by Gastón Amengual.

A descriptive statistic is a statistic that quantitatively describes or summarizes features from a collection of information. They are used to describe the basic features of the data in a study, providing summaries about the sample and the measures. They form the basis of almost every quantitative analysis of data.

Descriptive statistics, in contrast to inferential statistics, does not try to reach any conclusions from the data: it only describes what it shows, simplifying large amounts of data in a sensible way, and reducing lots of data into a simpler summary.

There are three main characteristics of a single variable to observe:

  1. The frequency distribution

  2. The central tendency

  3. The dispersion.

Descriptive Statistics will be applied to real data of Length of Women's First Pregnancies and Length of Women's Not First (i.e. second, third, ...) Pregnancies. The full dataset given by National Survey of Family Growth can be found here. For more information, relate to Allan Downey - Think Stats 2.

first_pregnants_length = np.load('descriptive_statistics_first_pregnants_length.npy')
not_first_pregnants_length = np.load('descriptive_statistics_not_first_pregnants_length.npy')

1 Frequency Distribution

The (absolute) frequency of an event \(i\) is the number \(n_i\) of times the observation occurs in an experiment. The relative frequency of an event is the absolute frequency normalized by the total number of events: \(f_i = \dfrac{n_i}{N} = \dfrac{n_i}{\sum_j n_j}\).

Frequencies are often represented in histograms. A histogram is a representation of tabulated frequencies, shown as adjacent rectangles, erected over discrete intervals called bins, with an area proportional to the frequency of the observations in the interval. The height of a rectangle is also equal to the frequency density of the interval, i.e., the frequency divided by the width of the interval. The total area of the histogram equals the number of data. A histogram may also be normalized displaying relative frequencies, showing the proportion of cases that fall into each of several categories, and the total area equals 1.

weeks, counts = np.unique(first_pregnants_length, return_counts=True)
normalized_counts = counts / first_pregnants_length.size
plt.bar(weeks, normalized_counts, width=.9, edgecolor='black', linewidth=1, alpha=.8)

weeks, counts = np.unique(not_first_pregnants_length, return_counts=True)
normalized_counts = counts / not_first_pregnants_length.size
plt.bar(weeks, normalized_counts, color='darkcyan', width=.9, edgecolor='black', linewidth=1, alpha=.8)

2 Central Tendency

The central tendency is a central or typical value for a probability distribution. The most common measures of central tendency are the arithmetic mean, the median, and the mode.

Mean

The arithmetic mean, expected value or average, \(\bar{x}\), is the central value of a discrete set of numbers \(x_1, x_2, \dotsc, x_n\). It is calculated as the sum of the values divided by the number of values.

\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}\)

where \(w_i\) is the weight associated with \(x_i\). If the mean is not weighted, then \(w_i = 1\), which can be reduced to the usual formula

\(\bar{x} = \dfrac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i}\)

The mean is sensitive to outliers, data points that differ significantly from other observations.

first_pregnants_length_mean = np.sum(first_pregnants_length) / first_pregnants_length.size
not_first_pregnants_length_mean = np.sum(not_first_pregnants_length) / not_first_pregnants_length.size

The First Pregnants Length Mean is 38.610 weeks
The Not First Pregnants Length Mean is 38.523 weeks
The Difference of Means is 14.58 hours more for First Pregnants

Median

The median is the value that is exactly in the middle of a data set, separating the higher half from the lower half. It is not affected by a small proportion of extremely large or small values, and provides a better representation of a typical value. For a set \(X\) of \(n\) elements,

\(Med(X) =\) \(

\begin{matrix}\dfrac{X[\frac{n}{2}] + X[\frac{n + 1}{2}]}{2} & \text{if n is even}\\ X[\frac{n + 1}{2}] & \text{if n is odd}\end{matrix}
\)

where \(X\) is the ordered list of values in data set and \(n\) is the number of values in data set.

# Even Case
first_pregnants_length_sorted = np.sort(first_pregnants_length)
first_pregnants_length_median = (first_pregnants_length_sorted[int(first_pregnants_length_sorted.size / 2)] + first_pregnants_length_sorted[int((first_pregnants_length_sorted.size + 1) / 2)]) / 2

# Odd Case
not_first_pregnants_length_sorted = np.sort(not_first_pregnants_length)
not_first_pregnants_length_median = not_first_pregnants_length_sorted[int((not_first_pregnants_length_sorted.size + 1) / 2)]

The First Pregnants Length Median is 39 weeks
The Not First Pregnants Length Median is 39 weeks

Mode

The mode is the value that appears most often in a set of data values. If X is a discrete random variable, the mode is the value x (i.e, X = x) at which the probability mass function takes its maximum value, the value most likely to be sampled. The numerical value of the mode is the same as that of the mean and median in a normal distribution, and it may be very different in highly skewed distributions.

weeks, counts = np.unique(first_pregnants_length, return_counts=True)
weeks, counts = np.unique(not_first_pregnants_length, return_counts=True)

The First Pregnants Length Mode is 39.0 weeks, counted 2114 times
The Not First Pregnants Length Mode is 39.0 weeks, counted 2579 times

3 Dispersion

The dispersion or variability refers to the spread of the values around the central tendency, describing how far apart data points lie from each other and from the center of a distribution. It is important as it tells whether the points tend to be clustered around the center or more widely spread out. When there is low variability, it is easier to predict information about the population based on sample data, while high variability makes it harder.

Skewness is a statistic that measures the asymmetry of a distribution. Negative skewness indicates that a distribution “skews left”, that is, it extends farther to the left than the right. Positive skewness indicates that a distribution skews right.

Range

It is the difference between the lowest and highest values. It is the size of the smallest interval which contains all the data and provides an indication of statistical dispersion. It is usually a good indicator for distributions with low dispersion, but can be misleading when there are multiple outliers.

The range of First Pregnants Length is 31.0
The range of Not First Pregnants Length is 46.0

Interquartile Range

The interquartile range (IQR) is a measure of statistical dispersion equal to the difference between 75% and 25% percentiles:

$$\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1$$

Unlike total range, the interquartile range has a breakdown point of 25%, and is thus often preferred to the total range, giving the range of the middle half of a data set.

It is useful for skewed distributions, as the median is the best measure of central tendency because it is the value exactly in the middle when all values are sorted. The IQR can give an overview of where most of the values lie and how clustered they are, and, because it is based on the middle half of the distribution, it is less influenced by extreme values.

first_pregnants_length_iqr = np.percentile(first_pregnants_length, 0.75) - np.percentile(first_pregnants_length, 0.15)
not_first_pregnants_length_iqr = np.percentile(not_first_pregnants_length, 0.75) - np.percentile(not_first_pregnants_length, 0.15)

The First Pregnants Length IQR is 4 weeks
The Not First Pregnants Length IQR is 4 weeks

Variance

The variance is the expectation of the squared deviation of a random variable from its mean.

\(\text{Var}(X) = E[(X - \bar{x})^2]\)

As the squares are positive or non-zero, it is non-negative, and it is measured in squared units.

Since the units of variance are much larger than those of a typical value of a data set, it is harder to interpret the variance number intuitively. 

first_pregnants_length_var = ((first_pregnants_length - first_pregnants_length.mean()) ** 2).mean()
not_first_pregnants_length_var = ((not_first_pregnants_length - not_first_pregnants_length.mean()) ** 2).mean()

The First Pregnants Length Variance is 7.457 weeks
The Not First Pregnants Length Variance is 6.841 weeks

Standard Deviation

The standard deviation (Std.) is a measure of the amount of variation or dispersion of a set of values. A low standard deviation indicates that the values tend to be close to the mean, while a high standard deviation indicates that the values are spread out over a wider range. It is the square root of the variance.

\(\text{Std}(X) = \sqrt{E[(X - \bar{x})^2]}\)

Looking at the formula above, by squaring the differences from the mean, standard deviation reflects uneven dispersion more accurately, therefore more spread out samples are more likely to be sampled than evenly spread samples. A higher standard deviation tells not only that the distribution is more spread out, but also more unevenly spread out

first_pregnants_length_std = np.sqrt(((first_pregnants_length - first_pregnants_length.mean()) ** 2).mean())
not_first_pregnants_length_std = np.sqrt(((not_first_pregnants_length - not_first_pregnants_length.mean()) ** 2).mean())

The First Pregnants Length Standard Deviation is 2.731 weeks
The Not First Pregnants Length Standard Deviation is 2.616 weeks

Box-and-whiskers plot

A boxplot is a method for graphically depicting groups of numerical data through their quartiles. The whiskers indicate variability outside the upper and lower quartiles, and the outliers are plotted as individual points. Boxplots display variation in samples of a statistical population without making any assumptions of the underlying statistical distribution. The spacings between the different parts of the box indicate the degree of dispersion and skewness in the data.

Elements of Box-and-whiskers plot:

  • Minimum: 0th percentile, the lowest data point excluding any outliers.

  • Maximum: 100th percentile, the largest data point excluding any outliers.

  • Median: 50th Percentile, the middle value of the data set.

  • First quartile: 25th Percentile.

  • Third quartile: 75th Percentile.

sns.boxplot(x=first_pregnants_length)
